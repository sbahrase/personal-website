PhD Thesis Highlights

My PhD study has been focused on one simple question, what is mass!?
The Standard Model of particle physics describes the fundamental particles and their interactions, it has withstood numerous tests over the past few decades, however, it has many open questions, such as the origin of mass, the source of dark energy and dark matter in our universe and many more ...

My research has been focused on two particles: 1) Higgs boson, it is the latest elementary particle discovered at LHC in 2012, all the particles of the Standard Model are believed to gain their mass through interactions with the Higgs field. 2) tau lepton, the heavy cousin of electron which is highly unstable (decay time $\approx 2.9\times10^{-13}s$). There are several compelling theories predicting more particles with characteristics same as the Higgs Boson. I have been search for one such particle through Petabytes of proton-proton collision data recorded by the ATLAS detector at CERN during 2015-2018.

The Large Hadron Collider, the world’s most powerful particle accelerator, is built to deepen our understanding of the building blocks of the universe and their underlying interactions. The $27km$ ring of superconducting magnets and accelerating components boost protons up to $0.999999991$ of the speed of light. At LHC proton beams are collided at a rate of roughly one billion per second. Advance particle detectors, like ATLAS record data from the myriad of particles produced from these collisions, amounting to about 100 Petabytes per year. The data and compute is then distributed over The Worldwide LHC Computing Grid (WLCG), an international grid-based computer network infrastructure incorporating over 170 computing centers around the globe.

Analysis Chain


  1. Proton-proton collision data is recorded by the ATLAS detector
  2. Baseline physics objects (electrons, photons, ...) are reconstructed and reduced data formats are made available for the next steps of the analysis
  3. Background processes which have the same signature as the process of interest (signal) are modelled, since they usually are orders of magnitude larger than signal their precise modelling is crucial
  4. Classifiers are developed to efficiently classify signal events from background ones
  5. Statistical interpretation of results is preformed to quantify any excess of events from the background processes in the light of he signal process

Machine Learning

Di-Tau Mass Reconstruction

Mass estimators are good tools in search for rare particle physics processes. In particular, as one of my early PhD projects, I have been developing Machine Learning (ML) techniques to estimate mass for a system of two tau leptons. Tau lepton has a very short life time and some of its decay products (neutrinos) can not be measured due to their extremely weak interactions with ordinary matter. The challenge is to estimate the mass of the system using only the visible decay products in a detector. The goal is to find $m_{\tau\tau} =? f(p^{\mu}_{\tau^{vis}_1}, p^{\mu}_{\tau^{vis}_2})$


This regression problem is solved through ML based methods such as Boosted Regression Trees (BRTs) and Neural Networks (NN). ditau-mass-calculators software built on top of ROOT and scikit-learn is written for this purpose.


While, the ML-based di-tau mass estimators are very competitive to other (classic) estimators performance-wise, the great advantage they are $\approx 3000$ times faster.

Search for Charged Higgs Boson

One of the primary challenges in search for rare particle physics processes is classifying proton-proton collision events to whether they are coming from background processes (not of main interest, but, very large) or from signal process which is of the main interest. In order to tackle this challenge in search for charged Higgs Bosons, using high level information from the detector (energy deposits, trajectory information, etc) ML-based methods are developed. A software on the top of ROOT and scikit-learn called hpana, is built. With meticulous attention to features selection, optimization and validation, Gradient Boosting Decision Trees (GBDTs) showed high efficiency in signal and background separation.

Tau Identification with Recurrent Neural Networks

Tau-lepton is one of the most elusive particles of the Standard Model, its reconstruction and identification is particularly difficult due to its very short decay time and invisible decay products (neutrinos). The decay signature (energy deposit, trajectory) of tau leptons is very similar to some other particles produced abundantly from proton-proton collisions at ATLAS. A rigorous identification algorithm is crucial for all the searches involving this particle. For this purpose BDT and Recurrent Neural Network (RNN)-based algorithms are developed to address challenges associated with tau identification. Reconstructed objects in the ATLAS detector can be viewed as an ordered sequence after imposing a physically-motivated ordering (e.g. ordering by object energy) and are thus suited for the use in recurrent neural networks. The RNN uses a combination of low-level input variables for individual tracks and clusters that are associated to the tau candidate as well as several high-level observables observables calculated from track and calorimeter quantities. To facilitate the use of associated charged particle tracks, clusters in the calorimeter and high-level variables, the network is split into three dedicated branches for each type of input. The high-level features associated to each tau lepton candidate are processed through a sequence of three fully connected layers with 128, 128 and 16 units.

Big Data & Cloud Computing

ATLAS experiment's current Event Data Model for analysis, called the xAOD, is based on the idea of the separation of the object data from the objects themselves (the ‘auxiliary store’). Rather than being stored as member variables of the analysis classes, all object data are stored separately, as vectors of simple values. Thus, the data are stored in a ‘structure of arrays’ format, while the user still can access it as an ‘array of structures’. The resulting data structures can be written to ROOT files with data properties represented as simple ROOT TTree branches .


Worldwide LHC Computing Grid (WLCG) provides the resources to store, distribute, and process the LHC data, over more than 170 data centers in 36 countries around the globe. Rucio , an open-source software developed by the ATALS collaboration is used to organize, manage, and access the data across heterogeneous data centers at widely distributed locations.

Production ANd Distributed Analysis (PanDA)

The PanDA system has been developed by ATLAS to meet the requirements for a data-driven workload management system for production and distributed analysis processing capable of operating at LHC data processing scale.

Quantum Computing

Quantum computers leverage quantum mechanical phenomena to manipulate information. To do this, they rely on quantum bits, or qubits. qubits Since qubits are quantum systems, we can capitalize on the phenomena of "superposition" and "entanglement."

  • Superposition: is naively the ability of a quantum system to be in multiple states at the same time

  • Entanglement: two or more quantum systems can be inextricably linked in perfect unison, even if separated by great distances.

To see how a simple addition can be done on a quantum computer see my recent presenation on this, also for the code please see this git repository.

My currents research in Quantum Computing is around developing quantum computing-based techniques for particles track reconstruction at the ATLAS experiment and Quantum Machine Learning. The ATLAS detectors is expected to be delivered a significantly higher proton-proton collision rate in a few years. This will make particles' track reconstruction using huge numbers of hits in different cells of the detector even more complicated and can hit serious computational limitations. Quantum computing speed up is one of the areas that ATLAS is seeking a solution from and I'm interested in. On the rather new Quantum Machine Learning frontier, I'm excited about implementation and interpretation of algorithms that can be used on real life problems.

ATLAS Offline Software

ATLAS software, also called Athena, is a framework that manages almost all ATLAS production workflows: event generation, simulation, reconstruction and derivation production. Written mainly in C++ and Python, with millions of lines of code, Athena is one of the most complicated software that I have ever contributed to.

xTau Framework

Starting from Derived xAOD data formats, xTauFramework, provides functionalities for the calibration, correction of physics objects, estimation of uncertainties, and event selections for several tau-related analyses The current output format is a ROOT TTree. Events for the Nominal case and kinematic variations are fully processed and stored in individual TTrees. Weight systematic variations are attached as branches onto the Nominal TTree. The xTauFramework strongly relies on C++ inheritance, polymorphism and common functionalities.

$H^{\pm} \to \tau\nu$ Search Software

hpana, dedicated analysis software for $H^{\pm} \to \tau\nu$ searches is written on top of the ROOT and scikit-learn. This Pythonic software utilizes OOP and parallel processing at large. Starting from ROOT TTrees, hpana provides various tools for modelling and validation of the background processes, training, optimization and evaluation of Boosted Decision Trees (and Neural Networks) for event classification. The outputs are histograms that are written to disk for statistical interpretation of the search at the latter steps.
